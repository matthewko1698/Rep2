South China Sea (link)

The first visualization that I want to analyze is by the South China Morning Post. This visualization presents the current territorial dispute between many Asian countries in the South China sea. Many people know a little about the dispute, especially between more vocal players like the Phillipines and China. Most people do not know the whole story, however. This interactive visualization helps people understand what countries are involved and how involved they are. The visual is simple and easy to use, and the information is based on truthful events that have occured. It is funcitional and simple to use, but it does not show how the events have taken place in time and how they have changed throughout the years. It is also very visually appealing. In terms of insightfulness, it is not very insightful because others who are knowledgeable about the situation can look at a map and visualize the claim dispute themselves. Cairo claims that a good visualization is also enlightening, but this visualization is not so enlightening as it is simply informative. It does not expand the shoreline of wonder because it does not give the reader incentive to continue exploring about the topic.

To improve the visualization, I think historical context is the most important addition that can be added. Understanding how the dispute began, and who the most important players were in the dispute is important but is not quite present in the visual itself. This addition would pull readers in and enlighten them on the situation, giving them enough information to make an educated opinion. A simple animation on movement or actions by each party would be helpful in setting the stage for self learning for the reader, instead of people giving others opinions about the situation without the whole story. Because the topic is relevant and important, a visualization that helps viewers understand the whole story would improve the visualization immensely. This visualization provides little to no trajectory into what will likely happen next or what has happened, only focusing on what is happening now.


EU Political shift

The second visualization I thought was great is a visualization of the plolitical shifts in the EU. The visualization is truthful and is based on the same years for all countries explored. The visualization is functional in that it tells a story about the countries backed by the data that the a shift toward the right has occured politcally and has changed over the years. The data is presented simply and beautifully and easily shows how the data supports their claim. It is insightful because it is generally difficult to quanitify how much political representation has changed over many years in a simple, quantified way. Lastly, it is enlightening because it is helps people to understand not only what has happened but gets the reader to wonder why this has occured. This visualization not only expands the island of knowledge but it expands the shorline of wonder because this data is only part of the story. This interactive, at least to me, encourages the reader to look into the situation more deeply, figuring out why people decided to think this way or what event caused a change in mindset. Because this is critical to many different political events, such as Brexit, it is very important to understand. This understanding can also be compared to other parts of the world, like the United States, where similar events have occured.
